Bangkok looks like a transport nightmare on a map. In practice, 80% of nomad life happens along the BTS line, and Grab handles the rest. You don't need a car. You don't need a scooter. You barely even need to walk far if you don't want to.

## The ranking

1. **BTS** — fastest, air-conditioned, runs above traffic. Default for anything in central Bangkok.
2. **MRT** — same idea underground. Connects east-west across the city.
3. **Grab car** — for short-medium trips, late nights, airport runs.
4. **Bolt** — backup ride-hail when Grab is surging.
5. **Motorbike taxis** — for the last mile (the bit between BTS and your condo). Cheap, fast, slightly terrifying.
6. **Tuk-tuks** — fun for first-time tourists. Overpriced for nomads.
7. **Regular metered taxis** — mostly fine, sometimes refuse meter. Always say "by meter, please" first.
8. **Walking** — only for short distances. 38°C + humidity makes 1km feel like 5km.

## BTS / MRT

### Cards

- **Rabbit Card (BTS)** — buy at any BTS station, top up at the kiosk. ~50 THB issue fee + your top-up.
- **MRT card** — separate card for the metro. Same idea, different system. Annoying that they don't combine.

### Fares

- BTS: 17–62 THB depending on distance. Single-trip in central Bangkok is usually 23–43 THB (about £0.50–£1).
- MRT: similar.

### Practical notes

- The BTS gets **packed** at 8–9am and 5–7pm. Avoid if you can.
- BTS shuts at midnight. After that, it's Grab or walking.
- Some areas (Thonglor, Ari) need a 10–15 min walk from the station to your final address.

## Grab

The killer app in Bangkok.

- **Grab Car** — standard ride.
- **Grab Premium** — newer cars, slightly more.
- **Grab Bike** — motorbike taxi, for short trips when traffic is hell.
- **Grab Food** — food delivery, ~30 min to most condos.
- **GrabPay** — link your card once, never touch cash again.

<ProTip>
For airport runs, pre-book your Grab the night before. At 5am there are fewer drivers; pre-booking guarantees pickup. For 4–5am flights, set a "scheduled" Grab.
</ProTip>

## Taxis

Metered pink/yellow/blue taxis are everywhere. They're often fine, but:

- They sometimes **refuse the meter** for foreigners. If a driver quotes a flat fare, say "meter, please" or walk to the next one.
- They sometimes claim they don't go to your destination. Common rip-off — usually they just don't want a short trip.
- Always have cash. Many older taxis don't take card.

## Motorbike taxis

The orange-vest guys at the corner of every soi.

- Used for the **last mile** — from BTS to your condo.
- Cheap: 20–60 THB.
- Wear the helmet they give you.
- Don't use them for long distances.

## Traffic reality

- Bangkok traffic is brutal between 3pm and 8pm.
- A 5km Grab can take 15 minutes or 75 minutes depending on time of day.
- Always check Google Maps for ETA before committing to a Grab over the BTS.
- If you're going from Sukhumvit to Khao San at 6pm in a car — don't. Take the boat or BTS + Grab.

## Airport transfers

- **Suvarnabhumi (BKK)**: Airport Rail Link to Phaya Thai or Makkasan (~45 THB), then taxi/BTS. Or Grab door-to-door (~300–500 THB depending on traffic).
- **Don Mueang (DMK)**: harder to reach by train. Use Grab (~200–400 THB) or the A1 bus to Mo Chit then BTS.

<WarningCard title="Tuk-tuks are not for getting around" severity="info">
They're a tourist experience, not a transport option. They'll quote you 200 THB for a trip that's 80 THB by Grab. Do one for fun, then never again.
</WarningCard>

## Scooters? Don't.

Every nomad who watches Bali YouTube videos arrives in Bangkok thinking they'll rent a scooter. Most of them shouldn't. This isn't a "you can't because it's dangerous" lecture — it's a "you genuinely don't need to, and the math is worse than you think" lecture.

### Bangkok is not the place to casually learn

Bangkok traffic is its own thing:

- Eight-lane roads with no clear lane discipline.
- Motorbike taxis weaving at 70 km/h between buses.
- Tourists wandering into traffic looking at their phones.
- Sudden monsoon rain that turns roads into rivers in 4 minutes.
- Police checkpoints that fine you on the spot for not having an international license.

If you've never ridden before, **Bangkok is the last place to learn**. Learn in Pai, Chiang Mai, or Koh Lanta. Then maybe ride in Bangkok if you ever want to.

### Licensing

You technically need:

1. A valid driving license from your home country **with a motorcycle endorsement**, OR
2. A Thai motorcycle license, OR
3. An **International Driving Permit (IDP)** that includes motorcycles.

A car-only license is **not enough**. If you're stopped and don't have the right paperwork, the fine is 500–1,000 THB on the spot. Worse — if you crash without the right license, **your travel insurance won't cover you**.

<WarningCard title="The insurance problem" severity="danger">
Most travel insurance policies (SafetyWing, World Nomads, IMG Global) only cover scooter accidents if you have a license that legally permits you to ride that vehicle. No license = no payout. A bad accident in Bangkok can be £20,000+ in medical bills. People go home in debt over this.
</WarningCard>

### The math

| Option | Cost / month | Hassle | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scooter rental | ~£100–£150 | Helmet + fuel + parking | High |
| BTS + Grab | ~£70–£100 | Zero | Low |
| Full Grab | ~£150 | Zero | Low |

You save almost nothing by riding a scooter. You take on real injury risk. You waste hours on parking and fuel runs.

### When scooters actually make sense

- **Outside Bangkok.** Chiang Mai, Pai, Koh Phangan, Koh Lanta — different story. Slower traffic, real need, lower stakes.
- If you're staying **6+ months** in a specific Bangkok area and commuting daily to a fixed spot off the BTS.
- If you already have **years of riding experience** and are willing to accept the risk.

<ProTip>
If you really want the "scooter freedom" thing — book a long weekend in **Pai or Koh Lanta** and rent there. You'll get all the joy with 1/10th the risk. Bangkok rewards public transport users.
</ProTip>
