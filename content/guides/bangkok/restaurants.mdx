Bangkok's food scene is staggering. You can eat better here for £3 than for £30 in most western capitals — but only if you know which £3 stalls and which £30 spots are worth it.

This is where we eat regularly, where we send friends visiting for a weekend, and the places that get hype they don't deserve.

<ProTip>
The best food in Bangkok is in places with no English menu and plastic chairs. Sukhumvit's "international" restaurants are mostly priced for expats — fine occasionally, never as your default.
</ProTip>

## Skip these

<WarningCard title="Sukhumvit Soi 11 'international row'" severity="warn">
The cluster of bar-restaurants on Soi 11 — Above Eleven, Charcoal Tandoor, etc. — exists for the tourist crowd. The food is mid, the prices are western, and the actual best Indian / Italian / Japanese in Bangkok is elsewhere.
</WarningCard>

<WarningCard title="Anything attached to a hotel buffet" severity="warn">
"All-you-can-eat" Thai buffets are designed for people who don't know any better. The good kitchens don't run buffets — they cook to order.
</WarningCard>

## Quick wins by area

- **Asoke / Sukhumvit:** _Cabbages & Condoms_ (yes really — the food's good and the proceeds fund family planning) · _Boon Tong Kiat Singapore Chicken Rice_
- **Thonglor:** _Soul Food Mahanakorn_ · _80/20 sister kitchen_ · the morning sois off 38 for street stalls
- **Ari:** _Lay Lao_ (Isaan / northeastern) · _Salt_ (Italian, the one exception to the rule)
- **Old town (Banglamphu / Tha Tien):** _Jay Fai_ · _Krua Apsorn_ · the boat noodle stalls north of the Grand Palace
- **Chinatown (Yaowarat):** any street stall after 7pm. Just walk and follow the queues.

<ProTip>
Set a personal rule: at least 3 street-stall meals a week. The day you stop is the day Bangkok becomes ordinary.
</ProTip>
